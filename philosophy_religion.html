Philosophy of Religion

A structured course in philosophical analysis of religious questions. No textbook required—all readings are free IEP and SEP materials.

Free, Open-Access Course

Course Goals

This course teaches you how philosophers analyze religious problems using conceptual clarity, argument structure, and attention to unresolved disagreement. It is not about personal belief—it is about method.

By the end of this course, you should be able to:

  • SLO 1: Identify philosophers and their theories
  • SLO 2: Understand forms of philosophical discourse
  • SLO 3: Understand core philosophical issues (metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, religion)
  • SLO 4: Construct philosophical arguments
  • SLO 5: Apply philosophical viewpoints to everyday life

📚 Course Modules

Follow the modules in order. Each contains readings, companion guides, and assignments.

Week 4: Ontological Arguments

Ontological arguments attempt to derive existence from concepts alone. This week focuses on argument structure, pressure points, and why these arguments remain philosophically significant even when rejected.

📖 What This Week Is About

Ontological arguments begin from reason alone rather than observation. Philosophers analyze whether concepts, definitions, and necessity claims can carry existential force.

✓ What To Do This Week (In Order)

  1. Read the IEP article Ontological Arguments
  2. Read the SEP Companion Sheet: Ontological Arguments
  3. Complete Argument Structure Analysis #1

Purpose: Understand how the argument works structurally.

SEP Companion Sheet: Ontological Arguments

Narrative Context: Unlike cosmological and teleological arguments, ontological arguments do not start from world-features like causation or design. They test the limits of logic, definition, and necessity.

Assigned Excerpts:

  1. What Ontological Arguments Are
  2. Why Ontological Arguments Are Difficult to Classify
  3. Uses and Goals of Ontological Arguments
  4. General Objections

What to Pull Out:

  • The defining feature that distinguishes ontological arguments from cosmological or teleological arguments
  • One reason philosophers still find ontological arguments interesting even when they reject them
  • One general type of objection raised against ontological arguments

How SEP Changes IEP: SEP highlights that ontological arguments are not only theological claims; they are also stress tests for logic and conceptual commitment.

Write one sentence: what SEP makes clearer than IEP.

Self-Check: Why do ontological arguments test the limits of reason more directly than other arguments for God's existence?

Assigned SEP Source: Ontological Arguments (instructor-selected excerpts only).

🎯 When You Are Done

  • Reconstruct the structure of an ontological argument
  • Identify key philosophical pressure points
  • Explain why failed arguments can remain philosophically important

Week 14: Religion and Violence

This week examines philosophical approaches to religion and violence by shifting the question from blame to mechanism. The emphasis is on moral framing, not accusation.

📖 What This Week Is About

Rather than asking whether religion causes violence, philosophers analyze how violence becomes morally intelligible within certain frameworks. The central mechanism is dehumanization, understood as moral exclusion.

The key question is not whether violence is wrong, but how moral obligations are weakened when others are no longer treated as full moral subjects.

✓ What To Do This Week (In Order)

  1. Read the IEP baseline framework Philosophy of Religion
  2. Read the SEP Companion Sheet: Dehumanization
  3. Complete Philosophical Tension Memo #5

Purpose: Resists simplistic narratives.

SEP Companion Sheet: Dehumanization

Narrative Context: Dehumanization is not unique to religion. It appears in religious, political, national, racial, and ideological contexts in structurally similar ways.

Core Idea to Track: Dehumanization denies or diminishes moral status and allows violence to appear justified, necessary, or permissible.

Conceptual Distinctions to Extract:

  • Animalistic dehumanization: representing others as beasts, savages, or subhuman
  • Mechanistic dehumanization: representing others as objects, tools, or functions
  • Moral status vs moral treatment: distinguish who counts as a moral subject from how people are treated in practice
  • Causal explanation vs moral justification: explaining violence does not morally license violence

One Required Check: What happens to moral obligations when someone is no longer perceived as fully human?

🎯 When You Are Done

  • Explain how dehumanization alters moral status
  • Distinguish causal explanation from moral justification
  • Analyze religion as one context among many where moral exclusion can occur

Course Learning Outcomes Addressed This Week: SLO 2 (philosophical discourse) and SLO 3 (core philosophical issues in religion).
